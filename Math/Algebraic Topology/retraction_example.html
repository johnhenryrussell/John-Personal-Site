Retraction Example

Def 1. A topology on a set \(X\) is a collection \(\mathcal{T}\) of subsets of \(X\) having the following properties

  1. \(\varnothing\) and \(X\) are in \(\mathcal{T}\)
  2. The union of the elements of any finite subcollection of \(\mathcal{T}\) is in \(\mathcal{T}.\)
  3. The intersection of the elements of any finite subcollection of \(\mathcal{T}\) is in \(\mathcal{T}.\)

Def 2. A set \(X\) for which a topology \(\mathcal{T}\) has been specified is called a topological space.

Def 3. Let \(X\) be a topological space and \(A\) a subspace of \(X\). Then a continuous map \[f: X \rightarrow A\] is a retraction if the restriction \(f |_A\) is the identity map on \(A\); that is, \(f(a) = a\) for all \(a \in A\). The subspace \(A\) is then called a retract of the original space \(X\).

One can also say that a retraction is a continuous mapping from a topological space into a subspace that preserves the positition of all points in that space.

Example: We can identify the torus \( (S^1 \times S^1)\) as a quotient space by taking \([0,1] \times [0,1]\) and identified edges of the square while preserving the arrow orientation.

It's less complicated than it sounds -- imagine connecting the \(A\) sides of the square to form a cylinder. Now, connect the two open ends of the cylinder to form a ring-like shape, i.e a torus.

Let \(X = S^1 \times S^1\). We can define \(M\) to be a line around the circumference of \(X\) and \(H\) to be a vertical cross section of \(X\). Notice that \(H\) intersects \(M\) at single point \(c\). Now, define a function \(r: X \rightarrow M \) where \(r(x)\) takes the point \(x\ \in H\) to the unique point \(c \in M\).


Let \(X = S^1 \times S^1\). We can define \(M\) to be a line around the circumference of \(X\) and \(H\) to be a vertical cross section of \(X\). Notice that \(H\) intersects \(M\) at single point \(c\). Now, define a function \(r: X \rightarrow M \) where \(r(x)\) takes the point \(x\ \in H\) to the unique point \(c \in M\).

It's clear that \(r\) is continuous since \(r^{-1}(U)\) is open when \(U\) is an open subset of \(X\). Furthermore, \(r(c)=c\) for all \(c \in M\). Hence, \(r\) is a retraction.
