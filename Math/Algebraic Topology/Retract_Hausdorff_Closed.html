Retraction of Hausdorff Space

Def 4. Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be topological spaces. The product topology on \(X \times Y\) is the topology having as basis the collection \(\mathbb{B}\) of all sets of the form \(U \times V\), where \(U\) is an open set of \(X\) and \(V\) is an open subset of \(Y\).

Def 1. A topology on a set \(X\) is a collection \(\mathcal{T}\) of subsets of \(X\) having the following properties

  1. \(\varnothing\) and \(X\) are in \(\mathcal{T}\)
  2. The union of the elements of any finite subcollection of \(\mathcal{T}\) is in \(\mathcal{T}\).
  3. The intersection of the elements of any finite subcollection of \(\mathcal{T}\) is in \(\mathcal{T}\).

Def 2. A set \(X\) for which a topology \(\mathcal{T}\) has been specified is called a topological space.

Def 3. A topological space \((X, \mathcal{T})\) is called Hausdorff if given two points \(x,y \in X\) and \(x \neq y \), there exists open disjoint sets \(U, V\) such that \(x \in U\) and \(y \in V\). A subspace of a Hausdorff space is also Hausdorff.

Def 4. Let \(X\) be a topological space and \(A\) a subspace of \(X\). Then a continuous map \[f: X \rightarrow A\] is a retraction if the restriction \(f |_A\) is the identity map on \(A\); that is, \(f(a) = a\) for all \(a \in A\). The subspace \(A\) is then called a retract of the original space \(X\).

One can also say that a retraction is a continuous mapping from a topological space into a subspace that preserves the positition of all points in that space.

Example: We can identify the torus \( (S^1 \times S^1)\) as a quotient space by taking \([0,1] \times [0,1]\) and identified edges of the square while preserving the arrow orientation.

It's less complicated than it sounds -- imagine connecting the \(A\) sides of the square to form a cylinder. Now, connect the two open ends of the cylinder to form a ring-like shape, i.e a torus.

Let \(X = S^1 \times S^1\). We can define \(M\) to be a line around the circumference of \(X\) and \(H\) to be a vertical cross section of \(X\). Notice that \(H\) intersects \(M\) at single point \(c\). Now, define a function \(r: X \rightarrow M \) where \(r(x)\) takes the point \(x\ \in H\) to the unique point \(c \in M\).

It's clear that \(r\) is continuous since \(r^{-1}(U)\) is open when \(U\) is an open subset of \(X\). Furthermore, \(r(c)=c\) for all \(c \in M\). Hence, \(r\) is a retraction.

Def 5. Let \(X \in \mathbb{R}\) and define a topology \(\mathcal{T}\) on \(X\). A set \(U \in \mathbb{R}\) is in \(\mathcal{T}\) if, given \(x \in U\), there is an \(\epsilon > 0\) such that \((x - \epsilon, x + \epsilon) \subset U\). This is called an open set.

A closed set is a set whose complement is open.

Problem: Prove that a retract of a Hausdorff space is closed.

Let's first draw a Hausdorff space.

Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be Hausforff topological spaces and define the product topology \(X \times Y\). By the definition of product topology, a point in \(X \times Y\) is of the form \((x,y)\) where \(x \in X\) and \(y \in Y\). This means that we can pick two distinct points \((a,b)\) and \((c,d)\) in \(X \times Y\) where \(a \neq c\) or \(b \neq d\) (or both).

Without loss of generality\(_1\), assume \(b \neq d\). Then \(V_1, \: V_2\) are two open disjoint sets such that \(b \in V_1\) and \(d \in V_2\). It follows that \(X \times V_1\) and \(X \times V_2\) are open disjoint sets in \(X \times Y\) such that \((a,b) \in X \times V_1\) and \((c,d) \in X \times V_2\). Thus, \(X \times Y\) is Hausdorff.

Q.E.D.

1."Without loss of generality" is used when subsequent cases are sufficiently similar to the chosen one in that proving them follows the same logic.
